Affixed Verbs in Latin, English, and German

by John Fischer

NOTE: This is a temporary draft! Final version to be published elsewhere after revisions.

Introduction

Latin employs prefixing to allow the production of words with additional shades of meaning; this is especially prominent in verbs. Many prefixed verbs have even taken on new shades of meaning beyond what would be implied solely by the root and prefix. This ‘grid’ of prefixed root verbs, with its thousands of distinct meanings, has been inherited (and often also partially reborrowed) by the Romance languages. The English language, having borrowed extensively from French and Latin, has also come to rely on the Latin prefixed verb system for a significant portion of its vocabulary.

The historical Continental West Germanic dialect continuum, in which different varieties’ lexicons have readily cross-pollinated, cultivated a prefixed verb system based on roots and prefixes of Germanic origin. Through contact with Middle Low German, especially during the Hanseatic period, the Scandinavian languages borrowed many continental Germanic words, including Germanic prefixed verbs.

English employs a few different verb affixing systems:

  • Germanic prefixed verbs (somewhat vestigial)
  • Phrasal verb system (also predominantly Germanic)
  • Latin prefixed verbs (borrowed extensively)

Each is presented in this article; in addition, the Germanic prefixed verb system of German is presented for comparison.

(To call an English phrasal verb an ‘affixed verb’ could be viewed as a stretch of terminology, as the ‘affix’ and the verb are separate words which might not even be adjacent in the sentence. This article chooses to accept the broadening of the term ‘affix’ to accommodate phrasal verbs.)

For each of the four affixed verb systems presented in this article, a table of words was cultivated over a couple months, with Wiktionary used to verify etymologies and to search for additional use cases of a given verb or prefix. For the German table, Duden online was occasionally used as well.

The tables presented here are to be taken as rough approximations. With each entry having been hand-checked in Wiktionary, coverage was prioritized over accuracy and consistency; errors are quite possible. These tables are intended to give an overall impression of the notable characteristics of the prominent Western European affixed verb systems.

Latin (via English loanwords)

The Latin prefixed verb system features prominently in the core vocabularies of the Romance languages and of English. Over the Medieval Latin and New Latin periods, new Latin words were synthesized from existing roots and affixes as the culture and science of Europe developed during the Middle Ages and the Renaissance. As Europeans increasingly wrote in their native languages rather than in Latin, Latin terms for scientific and technical innovations made their way into the spoken languages of Europe.

For some entries the etymologies are ambiguous or open to some interpretation. For instance, one can transform a Latin verb into frequentative form by adding endings to the supine stem that yield a new verb. An example is as follows:

  • speciō · specere · spexī · spectum (original)
  • spectō · spectāre · spectāvī · spectātum (frequentative)

The frequentative generally serves to marks an action as being repetitive; however, through Latin’s history, and notably in the development of the Romance languages, certain frequentatives would become widely used alongside and even in place of their originating verbs.

As noted in the Introduction, this table was made with coverage valued over accuracy and/or consistency. Words that are verbs in English are favored when possible, but in some cases English nouns and adjectives have been included.

This raises a question: why does this article focus solely on verbs? Indeed, adjectives or even nouns could be analyzed in this fashion as well. However, Latin has a quirk that makes analyzing verbs and adjectives together tricky: the prefix in-.

  • Sometimes Latin in- means ‘in’ or ‘inside’. This sense tends to be used with verbs, and is theorized to come from Proto-Indo-European *h1én.
  • Sometimes Latin in- is a negator. This sense tends to be used with adjectives, and is theorized to come from Proto-Indo-European *n̥-.

Some of these German verbs are separable; when a verb is separable, in some clause/phrase configurations the prefix is detached from the verb and placed at a separate position. In German the determination of whether a verb is separable tends to be vested in the prefix:

  • separable prefixes: ab-, an-, aus-, auf-, ein-, vor-, zurück-, weg-, mit-, los-, zusammen-
  • inseparable prefixes: ver-, be-, er-, ent-, ge-, zer-, miss-
  • mixed prefixes: durch-, unter-, um-

It’s tempting to draw parallels between German separable verbs and English phrasal verbs; while there are similarities, the two are not straightforwardly aligned. This will be explored in the next section.

English

Old English, whose lexicon was predominantly Germanic, had been favoring the use of Germanic prefixing for its verbs. Once English had turned to borrowing considerably from French and Latin, however, Latinate prefixed verbs became prominent.

In modern English there are still a few Germanic prefixed verbs; many of these concern finance and banking, and a few are recent coinages from computing.

Many of these accompanying particles are recognizable as adverbs and/or prepositions. In order to classify an accompanying particle of a phrasal verb, one may examine the syntactic constraints on the location of the particle in the relevant verb phrase/clause. In German, when a separable verb is used in separated form, the detached prefix appears as a particle at the end of the verb phrase/clause. With English phrasal verbs, by contrast, this is one potential outcome, but it is not universally permitted. This leads to a distinct concept of ‘separability’ for English phrasal verbs: whether or not the accompanying particle must appear immediately after the verb.

Further study

The four tables presented in this document, along with their accompanying explanations, only scratch the surface in describing the history and usage of affixed verbs in Germanic and Romance languages. Here are a few leads for further study.

Mixed-origin compounds

The tables above each use all-Germanic or all-Latinate roots and affixes to simplify the exposition; however, in a blended language such as English the appearance of mixed-original compounding is not too surprising. For instance, catch is used as the root verb for a few phrasal verbs, while around is used as the accompanying particle for several phrasal verbs.

In addition, calques, in which compound words are translated piece-by-piece in-place, can be considered a form of mixed origin: each piece of the word may have one origin while it is the manner of combination of those pieces that is of another origin. The tables above do not account for the possibility of calquing; that the verbs in the German table in particular are composed solely of Germanic roots and prefixes does not rule out the possibility that the inspiration for the coining of a given German prefixed verb came from a non-Germanic source (notably Latin).

Romance languages

The Latin-via-English table has considerable French influence; a table dedicated specifically to prefixed verbs in modern French would make for an interesting comparison. Tables could also be constructed for modern Spanish, Italian, and so on.

Germanic languages

With the varieties of German and the varieties of Dutch having historically formed a large dialect continuum, it’s not always straightforward to delineate what is a language or a dialect even today; one could conduct a comparison of prefixed verb systems across modern German and Dutch varieties.

The maritime trade of the Hanseatic League led to considerable borrowing of the Middle Low German prefixed verb system into Scandinavian languages.
